Certainly! Below is a Modelica model called `ClassicModel` that simulates the dynamics of a predator-prey system using the specified parameters and differential equations.

```modelica
model ClassicModel
  // Parameters
  parameter Real alpha = 0.1 "Reproduction rate of prey";
  parameter Real beta = 0.02 "Mortality rate of predator per prey";
  parameter Real gamma = 0.4 "General mortality rate of predator";
  parameter Real delta = 0.02 "Reproduction rate of predator per prey";

  // State variables
  Real x(start=10) "Prey population";
  Real y(start=10) "Predator population";

equation
  // Differential equations
  der(x) = alpha * x - beta * x * y;
  der(y) = delta * x * y - gamma * y;
end ClassicModel;
```

### Explanation:
- **Parameters**: These are constants that define the rates of reproduction and mortality for both prey and predators.
  - `alpha`: Reproduction rate of prey.
  - `beta`: Mortality rate of predator per prey.
  - `gamma`: General mortality rate of predator.
  - `delta`: Reproduction rate of predator per prey.

- **State Variables**: These represent the populations of prey (`x`) and predators (`y`), with initial values set to 10 for both.

- **Equations**:
  - `der(x) = alpha * x - beta * x * y`: This equation models the change in prey population. The prey population grows at a rate proportional to its current size (`alpha * x`) and decreases due to predation (`beta * x * y`).
  - `der(y) = delta * x * y - gamma * y`: This equation models the change in predator population. The predator population grows based on the availability of prey (`delta * x * y`) and decreases due to natural mortality (`gamma * y`).

This model is a classic representation of the Lotka-Volterra equations, which are used to study the dynamics of biological systems in which two species interact, such as a predator and its prey.
