
Create a Modelica model called `ClassicModel`. This model simulates the dynamics of a predator-prey system using a set of equations. The model should include parameters for the reproduction rate of the prey (alpha=0.1), the mortality rate of the predator per prey (beta=0.02), the general mortality rate of the predator (gamma=0.4), and the reproduction rate of the predator per prey (delta=0.02), each with specified initial values. Include two state variables, x for prey population and y for predator population, with initial conditions set from the parameters x0=10 and y0=10, respectively. The population change over time is governed by the following differential equations:
1. Prey Population (x): The growth is determined by its reproduction rate (alpha) minus the losses from predation, which is proportional to the encounter rate with predators (beta * y).
2. Predator Population (y): The growth depends on the consumption of prey (delta * x) minus its natural mortality rate (gamma).
Ensure both rates of change (der(x) and der(y)) are multiplied by their respective current populations.. This model is typically used to understand the dynamics of ecosystems and study population control strategies.

//modelica
```model ClassicModel
  parameter Real alpha=0.1 "reproduction rate of the prey";
  parameter Real beta=0.02 "mortality rate of the predator per prey";
  parameter Real gamma=0.4 "general mortality rate of the predator";
  parameter Real delta=0.02 "reproduction rate of the predator per prey";
  parameter Real x0=10 "initial prey population";
  parameter Real y0=10 "initial predator population";

  Real x(start=x0) "prey population";
  Real y(start=y0) "predator population";

equation
  der(x) = alpha*x - beta*x*y;
  der(y) = delta*x*y - gamma*y;
end ClassicModel;```

This model can be used to simulate the dynamics of a predator-prey system, where the prey population (x) grows at a rate determined by its reproduction rate (alpha) minus the losses from predation, which is proportional to the encounter rate with predators (beta * y), and the predator population (y) grows at a rate determined by the consumption of prey (delta * x) minus its natural mortality rate (gamma). The initial conditions for the populations are set by x0 and y0.

The model is simple and captures the basic dynamics of a predator-prey system. However, it does not include factors such as environmental changes, spatial distribution, or other interactions between species, which could be important in more complex ecosystems.
