
Suppose we have a simple vibration system consisting of a spring, a damper, and a mass (`m`) that moves in a vertical direction without external forces (`F0` = 0). The spring coefficient (`k`) is 1, the damping coefficient (`c`) is 0.1, and the particle mass (`m`) is 1. Please help me implement the Modelica model named `SpringMassDamper` corresponding to the above system. Define variables for displacement (`x`, starting at 1), velocity (`v`), and the resultant force applied to the mass (`F`). The equations should describe the resultant force (`F`) based on the spring and damping effects, and use Newton's second law to relate this force to the derivative of velocity, while also defining the derivative of displacement.

//modelica
```model SpringMassDamper
  parameter Real m = 1 "Mass";
  parameter Real k = 1 "Spring coefficient";
  parameter Real c = 0.1 "Damping coefficient";
  Real x(start=1) "Displacement";
  Real v "Velocity";
  Real F "Resultant force";
equation 
  F = m * der(v) + c * v + k * x;
  der(x) = v;
end SpringMassDamper;
```

This Modelica model describes a simple spring-mass-damper system. The force `F` is the resultant force applied to the mass, which is the sum of the force due to the spring (`k * x`) and the damping force (`c * v`). According to Newton's second law, this force is equal to the mass `m` times the acceleration of the mass, which is the time derivative of the velocity `v`. The equation for the derivative of the displacement `x` is also included, which is simply the velocity `v`.

Please note that the initial conditions for displacement `x` and velocity `v` are set to 1 as per your request. You may need to adjust these initial conditions depending on the specific requirements of your system.

Also, the parameters `m`, `k`, and `c` are defined at the top of the model and are used throughout the model. These parameters represent the mass of the particle, the spring coefficient, and the damping coefficient, respectively.

This model should work for a simple spring-mass-damper system without external forces. If you need to account for external forces or more complex dynamics, you may need to adjust the model accordingly.
