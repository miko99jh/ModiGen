
Generate a Modelica model named `Capacitor` for a DRY capacitor within an electrical system. The model should have a capacitance parameter 'C' with units in Farads. This capacitor extends the 'ModelicaByExample.Components.Electrical.DryApproach.TwoPin' model, functioning to relate the current 'i' and the voltage rate of change 'der(v)' through the equation 'C*der(v) = i'. Ensure accurate representation of this component's functionality and parameters in the system.

//modelica
```model Capacitor
  extends ModelicaByExample.Components.Electrical.DryApproach.TwoPin;
  parameter Modelica.SIunits.Capacitance C "Capacitance";
equation
  C*der(v) = i;
end Capacitor;
```

In this model, the capacitor extends the TwoPin model from ModelicaByExample.Components.Electrical.DryApproach. The TwoPin model provides the basic structure for a two-pin electrical component. The parameter 'C' represents the capacitance of the capacitor, with units in Farads. The equation 'C*der(v) = i' represents the relationship between the current 'i' and the rate of change of voltage 'der(v)', which is a fundamental law of electrical circuits.

In the equation, 'der(v)' represents the rate of change of voltage 'v' across the capacitor, and 'i' is the current flowing through the capacitor. The capacitance 'C' is a constant that determines the amount of charge 'q' that the capacitor can store for a given voltage 'v'. The equation 'C*der(v) = i' states that the rate of change of voltage is equal to the inverse of the capacitance times the current, which is the definition of capacitance.

This model can be used in an electrical system to accurately represent the behavior of a capacitor. The capacitance 'C' can be adjusted to model different capacitors with different amounts of capacitance. The current 'i' and the rate of change of voltage 'der(v)' can be calculated from the rest of the electrical system, and the equation 'C*der(v) = i' can be used to calculate the voltage 'v' across the capacitor.

Please note that this model assumes a linear capacitor with no internal resistance. For a more realistic model, you may need to include an internal resistance to account for the resistance of the dielectric material in the capacitor.
